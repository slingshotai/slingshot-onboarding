# The Story Overlap

The Story Overlap is the central principle underpinning the **Story** component of the [[The Slingshot Framework Overview|Slingshot Framework]]. It defines where all brand messaging should operate and applies to every touchpoint where a brand meets its customer -- copy, packaging, imagery, experience, and beyond.

**Core principle:** Your brand messaging only matters where your story and your customer's story intersect. Everything outside that overlap is noise.

**Source material:** EP Solo Episode -- "Why Your Customers Don't Care About Your Brand (And What They Care About Instead)" (March 2026). See [[EP Solo - Mar 2026 - Brand Story - Content Bundle]] for full script, blog post, and freebie spec.

---

## The Concept

Picture a Venn diagram. Two overlapping circles.

- **Circle 1 -- Your Brand Story:** Who you are, what you stand for, why you do what you do. Your history, your awards, your founding year, your values, your mission.
- **Circle 2 -- Your Customer's Story:** Their needs, desires, problems. The movie they're playing in their head when they come to your website. Their aspirations, frustrations, and the outcome they're hoping for.
- **The Overlap:** The sliver in the middle where the two circles meet. This is the only part that matters.

Customers care profoundly about their story but very little about ours. Our company history, our awards, our team photos -- that's all our circle. The customer doesn't see the hours we put into building it. The goal of all brand messaging is to operate in the overlap.

This isn't about diminishing our story. It's about recognising that successful marketing means coming to the painful realisation that customers are too focused on their own story to care much about ours.

---

## The Diagnostic: We vs You Ratio

A quick way to assess whether any page operates in the overlap:

1. Pick any page on your site (homepage, about page, product page)
2. Count every instance of "we," "our," and "us"
3. Count every instance of "you" and "your"
4. Calculate the ratio

**If the ratio is more than 1:1 in favour of "we," the page is too brand-centric.** It's operating from your circle, not the overlap.

### The Accountant Exercise

This is the teaching exercise used to demonstrate the principle:

1. Google "accountants near me" and pick any result
2. Open their homepage
3. Count the we:you ratio

In the original exercise, the ratio was 2.6:1 -- for every time they mentioned the customer, they mentioned themselves two and a half times. The page opened with the firm's name, followed by accreditation badges, followed by "we" language throughout. Everything was from the brand's circle. The customer's circle was almost entirely empty.

This exercise works because using accountants (not the listener's own industry) lets people be objective before the penny drops about their own site.

**Observation:** The more established the brand, the smaller the logo on the website. Apple's logo is tiny on their site. Because they've learned it's not about them.

## Finding the Overlap: Three Steps

### Step 1: Define Your Brand Story

Write your brand story in one paragraph. Not your history. Not your awards. Your **WHY**.

Guided prompts:
- Why does your company exist? (Not what you sell -- why you sell it.)
- What do you believe about your industry that most companies don't?
- If your brand were a person, what would they fight for?
- Complete this sentence: "We exist because we believe..."

Use emotive language, not corporate speak. Not "increasing shareholder value" -- something real.

Also define three brand attributes -- words that describe the brand's intangible qualities (tone, voice, feel). Not what you sell, but how it feels to interact with you.

**If Step 1 feels hard, start with Step 2.** Customer reviews will tell you more about your brand story than any brainstorming session. Do the review mining first, and Step 1 becomes much clearer.

**Worked example (JBC):** "We exist because we believe every person is remarkable, and we wanted to create a beauty experience that tells people that truth, not the lie that the beauty industry usually sells."

### Step 2: Map Your Customer's Story

Three exercises, in order of concreteness:

#### 2a: Review Mining (start here)

Pull 20 customer reviews -- a mix of 5-star, 3-star, and 1-star. Look for:
- **WHY** people buy, not just what they buy
- What words and phrases they use
- What emotions come through
- What they mention that you never expected

Extract the top 10 phrases/words customers use. These are your **overlap words** -- language that bridges your story and theirs.

AI can accelerate this significantly. Feed reviews into a prompt that analyses them through Jobs-to-be-Done (functional, emotional, social jobs) and extracts emotionally charged language.

**Worked example (JBC):** Review mining revealed customers weren't buying beauty products. They were buying a gift for themselves. A treat. Their special thing. This single insight changed everything.

#### 2b: Image Buckets

Gather 15-20 images that visually represent your ideal customer's world:
- Morning routine
- Workspace
- Leisure time
- Relationships
- Aspirations
- Frustrations

Visual clues reveal insights that demographics miss. This can also be deepened using Maslow's Hierarchy as a lens (safety needs, belonging, esteem, self-actualisation) to identify what images to gather at each level.

#### 2c: The Customer's Movie

Describe the scene: your customer is about to visit your website.
- Where are they?
- What are they doing?
- What problem are they trying to solve?
- What outcome are they hoping for?
- What are they afraid of?

Write a 2-3 sentence snapshot of that moment.

### Step 3: Find and Articulate the Overlap

Draw the Venn diagram. Literally. Brand story on one side, customer story on the other.

Guided prompts:
- What do you believe that your customer also believes?
- What do you care about that your customer also cares about?
- Where does your WHY meet their WANT?

**Write the overlap in a single sentence.** This becomes the foundation of every message you create.

**Worked example (JBC):**
- Brand circle: "Believe people are remarkable. Reject manipulated beauty standards. Want to create authentic experiences."
- Customer circle: "Want to treat themselves. Take care of their beauty. Don't want to be told they aren't good enough."
- Overlap: "Authentic beauty. You are remarkable as you are. Self-care without self-criticism."
- Overlap sentence: "Happy. Remarkable. You."

## Applying the Overlap

The overlap isn't just a messaging concept. It's an operating principle that applies everywhere the brand meets the customer.

### Copy and Headlines

**The Netflix Headline Formula -- Verb + Object + Sexiness:**
- Netflix: "Unlimited movies, TV shows, and more. Watch anywhere. Cancel anytime."
- Not a single "we" in sight. No mention of streaming technology or founding story. Just what the customer wants: content, freedom, no risk.
- Watch = Verb. Movies + TV Shows = Object. Unlimited = Sexiness.

This formula inherently forces overlap thinking because it centres on what the customer gets.

**Feature -> Benefit -> Outcome (Causal Sequencing):**
This is the connection to [[EP Solo - Mar 2026 - Brand Story - Content Bundle|Narrative Binding]]. Product copy that follows Feature -> Benefit -> Outcome works because it operates in the overlap. The feature is the brand's story. The outcome is the customer's story. The benefit bridges the two.

### Product Descriptions

The classic example: when Apple launched the iPod, every competitor talked specs ("5GB hard drive," "FireWire connectivity"). Apple said "1,000 songs in your pocket."

- "5GB of memory" = Apple's story (the technical achievement)
- "1,000 songs in your pocket" = the customer's story (their music, everywhere, easily)

Both describe the same product. One operates in the overlap. The other doesn't.

### Homepage

Most homepages are brochures about the company. They should be gateways to solving the customer's problem. Run the we:you audit. If the ratio favours "we," rewrite from the overlap.

**Before:** "Welcome to Jonathan Ford & Co. We're one of the friendliest firms of accountants. Our aims are pretty simple."
**After:** "Running a business is hard enough without worrying about your books. Get your finances sorted so you can focus on what you actually care about."

### About Page

Consistently in the top five most visited pages on ecommerce sites. Even the about page should ultimately be about the customer. Use phrases like "We exist because..." and lead straight to the customer's story.

### Packaging and Physical Experience

Packaging is where the overlap becomes tangible. The principle of **sensation transference** (Louis Cheskin) means people transfer their feelings about the packaging onto the product itself. He proved it with 7-Up -- same drink, different coloured can, people thought the flavour had changed.

**JBC progression:**
1. Started shipping in jiffy bags (standard, cheap)
2. Customers complained about damaged outer boxes (bashed tin of beans principle)
3. Switched to strong brown boxes (problem solved, but missed the opportunity)
4. Realised customers were buying a gift for themselves, not beauty products
5. Wrapped orders in tissue paper (unboxing = unwrapping a gift)
6. Added popcorn-shaped biodegradable packing pieces (fun, sustainable, shareable)
7. Added "Happy. Remarkable. You." messages inside every box

Result: customers started posting on social media. The packaging became remarkable -- worth remarking about (Seth Godin sense). Research shows positive unboxing experiences drive 50% higher repeat purchase rates and ~40% social sharing.

### Imagery and Environment

JBC exercise: asked salon staff to bring every image on display and keep any that weren't digitally altered. They couldn't keep a single one. Not one genuine, unaltered image of a real person.

Replaced magazines with Time's photo books -- real photos of real people. Customer feedback was immediate: "There's something different about this place. Something nicer." Without being told what had changed. Applied the same principle to the website.

The overlap: customers wanted to take care of their beauty but didn't want to be constantly told they weren't good enough. The brand didn't want to tell them that either. Where those two stories met was the magic.

### Social Media

Are you posting from your circle or from the overlap? Every post should be evaluated: does this serve the customer's story, or just ours?

## Small Brand Example: Hiut Denim

The overlap applies at any scale. Hiut Denim -- about fifteen people in Wales making jeans.

- Their brand story: craft, reviving their town's denim tradition
- The customer's story: wanting something made with care that actually lasts
- Their overlap message: "We make the best jeans, not the most jeans."

That tagline sits right in the intersection.

## The Storytelling Connection

Understanding story structure makes us better at communicating with customers. The Lee Child technique of showing the effect before the cause creates micro-suspense:

- "Donny spilled his coffee and had to change his shirt." (Informative, no suspense)
- "Donny had to change his shirt, because he spilt coffee over it." (Micro-mystery -- reader leans in)

This matters for the overlap because when we understand how the brain leans into story, we can pull customers into the narrative. And the narrative they'll always lean into is their own. The overlap is where we take our story and make it feel like their story.

## The Overlap Audit Checklist

Apply to any customer-facing touchpoint:

- [ ] **Homepage:** Does it speak to the overlap or just the brand?
- [ ] **About page:** Does it lead back to the customer's story?
- [ ] **Product descriptions:** Do they use Feature -> Benefit -> Outcome?
- [ ] **Headlines/hooks:** Verb + Object + Sexiness -- in the customer's language?
- [ ] **Packaging:** Does the physical experience match the emotional overlap?
- [ ] **Social media:** Posting from the brand circle or from the overlap?
- [ ] **We vs You ratio:** Less than 1:1 in favour of "we"?
